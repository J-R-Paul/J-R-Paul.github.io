Testing weak dependence and stationarity by Universal Inference

With Arnab Bhattacharjee and Mark E. Schaffer (2025)

We present a sequential Universal Inference (UI) test for detecting strong spatial dependence and non-stationarity in dynamic panel data with expanding dimensions N and T. Starting from a general spatial autoregressive (SAR) model with both contemporaneous and lagged spatial effects, we form incremental likelihood-ratio factors between an unrestricted quasi-MLE and the null of zero contemporaneous and lagged spatial coefficients. The cumulative product is a non-negative martingale with unit mean under the null, yielding an any-time, finite-sample level-α test via Ville’s inequality; inverting it gives finite-sample confidence sets for the SAR parameters. Monte-Carlo evidence shows strong size and power, especially near the unit-root boundary; we outline robust extensions and an asymptotic refinement for exact asymptotic level-α.

Uniform certification of fairness in algorithmic policy audits (working title)

We study the certification problem: with high probability, can we rule out large disparities across any metric–subgroup pair? Let θh be the disparity for pair h and H a possibly large family. We propose a simple sample-splitting procedure that estimates each θh on one split and uses the other to quantify the maximal estimation error across all h ∈ H, delivering a single, finite-sample upper bound on the worst-case disparity maxh |θh|. The bound is distribution-free, transparent, and computationally light, making it suitable for compliance checks and fairness audits.

Connections: relates to multiple testing (Bonferroni/Holm/stepdown), selective inference, and empirical process theory; it synthesizes ghost-sample/symmetrization ideas to provide exact certificates over arbitrary bounded metrics and combinatorial subgroup families.

Decision Breakdown Frontier: Certifying Policy Robustness to Ambiguity

This paper formalizes the decision breakdown frontier (DBF): an interpretable certificate of how much model ambiguity is required to overturn a policy recommendation. Starting from a baseline model P0 and loss ℓ, we study distributionally robust risk within Wasserstein balls of radius ε and define the decision breakdown radius (DBR) between actions a1 and a0 as the largest ε such that the robust risk gap Δ(ε) = V(a1; ε) − V(a0; ε) ≤ 0. We derive general properties of ε*, provide closed-form expressions under an asymptotically maximally Lipschitz (AML) condition, and give safe lower bounds without AML. We discuss centering, links to identification breakdown frontiers, computation, and inference, with logistic and absolute-deviation illustrations.
